Glossary

Key terms and concepts in attachment theory and relational healing
A
Adult Attachment Interview (AAI)
A semi-structured clinical interview developed by Mary Main and colleagues that assesses an adult's state of mind regarding attachment. The AAI evaluates not what people say about their childhoods but how coherently and reflectively they narrate their early experiences.
A person who describes their childhood as "perfect" but cannot provide specific supporting memories may be classified as dismissing on the AAI.
Affect Regulation
The ability to manage and modulate emotional experiences so they remain within a tolerable range. A core function of secure attachment is to develop the neurological capacity for affect regulation through repeated experiences of co-regulation with a caregiver.
A child who has been consistently soothed when distressed gradually internalizes the ability to soothe themselves.
Ainsworth, Mary
Canadian-American developmental psychologist (1913–1999) who developed the Strange Situation procedure and identified the first three attachment classifications: secure, anxious-ambivalent, and avoidant. Her work provided the empirical foundation for Bowlby's attachment theory.
Anxious-Preoccupied Attachment
An insecure attachment style characterized by hyperactivation of the attachment system, chronic worry about the availability of attachment figures, heightened sensitivity to signs of rejection, and protest behaviors designed to maintain closeness. Develops in response to inconsistently responsive caregiving.
An adult who becomes intensely distressed when their partner is slow to respond to messages and who cannot concentrate on other tasks until reassured.
Attachment Behavioral System
The innate, biologically based system that motivates proximity-seeking behavior toward attachment figures, especially during times of distress. Bowlby proposed this system evolved to promote survival by keeping vulnerable young close to protective caregivers.
Attachment Figure
A person who serves as a primary source of safety, comfort, and protection. In infancy, this is typically a parent or primary caregiver. In adulthood, romantic partners, close friends, therapists, and mentors can serve as attachment figures.
Attunement
The caregiver's ability to perceive, accurately interpret, and respond to a child's emotional and physical states. Attunement involves both affect matching (reflecting the child's emotional state) and affect regulation (helping the child modulate intense emotions).
A mother who notices her infant's subtle signs of overstimulation and gently reduces the level of interaction is demonstrating attunement.
Avoidant-Dismissive Attachment
An insecure attachment style characterized by deactivation of the attachment system, discomfort with emotional closeness, excessive self-reliance, and minimization of the importance of relationships. Develops in response to emotionally unavailable or rejecting caregiving.
An adult who values independence above all else, keeps partners at emotional arm's length, and describes their childhood as "fine" with little elaboration.
B
Bowlby, John
British psychiatrist and psychoanalyst (1907–1990) who developed attachment theory. His trilogy—Attachment, Separation, and Loss—provided the theoretical framework for understanding how early bonds with caregivers shape emotional development and relational functioning across the lifespan.
Boundaries
The limits and guidelines a person establishes to protect their physical, emotional, and psychological well-being within relationships. Healthy boundaries are flexible, clearly communicated, and serve the health of both the individual and the relationship.
"I love spending time with you, and I also need some evenings to myself" is a boundary that honors both connection and autonomy.
C
Coherent Narrative
A reflective, integrated account of one's life history that acknowledges both positive and difficult experiences without minimization or overwhelm. The capacity to construct a coherent narrative is the hallmark of earned secure attachment on the Adult Attachment Interview.
Co-Regulation
The mutual, bidirectional process by which one person's nervous system helps regulate another's. In secure attachment, co-regulation is the primary mechanism through which emotional distress is soothed. It begins in infancy and continues throughout life in close relationships.
A distressed child calming in a parent's arms, or a partner's steady breathing helping to slow the other's racing heart during anxiety.
Corrective Emotional Experience
A term coined by psychoanalyst Franz Alexander describing the healing that occurs when a current relationship provides the attunement, responsiveness, or safety that was absent in one's early attachment experiences, thereby updating the internal working model.
D
Deactivating Strategies
The defensive mechanisms used by avoidantly attached individuals to suppress awareness of attachment needs and maintain emotional distance. These include minimizing the importance of relationships, idealizing self-sufficiency, and inhibiting emotional expression.
Mentally cataloging a partner's flaws after a moment of genuine closeness is a deactivating strategy that restores emotional distance.
Disorganized-Fearful Attachment
An attachment pattern characterized by the absence of a coherent strategy for managing attachment needs. Develops when the caregiver is simultaneously a source of comfort and a source of fear, creating an irresolvable conflict in the child's attachment behavioral system.
An adult who intensely craves intimacy but sabotages relationships when they become close, oscillating between pursuit and flight.
Dissociation
A disruption in the normal integration of consciousness, memory, identity, emotion, perception, and behavior. In attachment contexts, dissociation often occurs as a response to overwhelming relational stress, particularly in disorganized attachment. It can range from mild (spacing out) to severe (loss of awareness of surroundings).
Dorsal Vagal Response
The oldest branch of the vagus nerve, associated with immobilization, shutdown, and the freeze or collapse response. In polyvagal theory (Stephen Porges), dorsal vagal activation represents the most primitive defense against overwhelming threat. In attachment, it corresponds to the hypoarousal state.
E
Earned Secure Attachment
A classification on the Adult Attachment Interview indicating that an individual who experienced insecure attachment in childhood has developed, through therapy, meaningful relationships, or reflective inner work, a coherent narrative and a secure state of mind regarding attachment. Functionally equivalent to continuous security.
Emotional Fusion
A state in which one's own emotional experience becomes indistinguishable from another person's. Common in anxious attachment, where the partner's mood becomes the primary determinant of one's own emotional state, creating excessive dependence on external regulation.
Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)
A structured approach to couples therapy developed by Sue Johnson, grounded in attachment theory. EFT helps partners identify the negative interaction cycles driven by their attachment needs and fears, access underlying vulnerable emotions, and create new patterns of secure bonding.
F
Felt Sense
A term from somatic psychology referring to the holistic, bodily-experienced knowing that encompasses physical sensation, emotion, and meaning simultaneously. In somatic experiencing, tracking the felt sense is the primary method for accessing and resolving held trauma.
The tightness in your chest when a partner raises their voice is a felt sense that carries both physical sensation and emotional meaning.
Fright Without Solution
Mary Main's term for the core experience of disorganized attachment, in which the child faces the irresolvable paradox of needing to approach the attachment figure for safety while simultaneously needing to flee from them as a source of fear.
G
Good-Enough Parent
A concept from Donald Winnicott describing the caregiver who is not perfect but is "good enough"—sufficiently attuned and responsive to meet the child's needs most of the time, while also allowing manageable frustrations that promote the child's developing autonomy and resilience.
Grounding
A set of techniques designed to bring awareness back to the present moment and the physical body, particularly useful when experiencing dissociation, anxiety, or emotional flooding. Grounding practices activate the ventral vagal system and counter the dissociative pull of trauma responses.
The "5-4-3-2-1" exercise (naming five things you see, four you touch, three you hear, two you smell, one you taste) is a widely used grounding technique.
H
Hyperactivation
The intensification of attachment behaviors in response to perceived threats to the attachment bond. The central mechanism of anxious attachment, involving heightened vigilance for signs of rejection, amplified emotional expression, and persistent proximity-seeking.
Hyperarousal
A state of nervous system activation above the window of tolerance, characterized by anxiety, panic, racing thoughts, hypervigilance, and fight-or-flight responses. In attachment contexts, hyperarousal is the characteristic stress response of the anxiously attached.
Hypoarousal
A state of nervous system activation below the window of tolerance, characterized by numbness, emotional flatness, dissociation, fatigue, and the freeze or collapse response. In attachment contexts, hypoarousal is associated with avoidant and disorganized patterns.
I
Implicit Memory
Memories stored outside of conscious awareness, typically encoded in the first two years of life before the development of explicit (autobiographical) memory. Attachment patterns are largely stored as implicit memories—felt, procedural, and somatic rather than verbal and narrative.
Inner Working Model
Bowlby's term for the mental representations, formed through early attachment experiences, that shape expectations about the self ("Am I worthy of love?"), others ("Are others reliable and responsive?"), and relationships ("Is intimacy safe?"). These models operate largely outside consciousness and guide relational behavior throughout life.
Internal Family Systems (IFS)
A therapeutic model developed by Richard Schwartz that views the psyche as composed of multiple "parts"—including protective parts and exiled, wounded parts—organized around a core Self. IFS is particularly useful for inner child and self-attachment work in the context of attachment healing.
Internal Secure Base
The internalized felt sense of safety and self-worth that develops through either continuous secure attachment or earned security. An internal secure base allows a person to self-soothe, tolerate uncertainty, and approach relationships from a place of choice rather than desperation.
K
Kintsugi
The Japanese art of repairing broken pottery with gold, rendering the mended piece more beautiful than the original. Used metaphorically in attachment literature to describe how the process of rupture and repair can strengthen relational bonds, with the repaired places becoming the most luminous seams.
M
Mentalization
The ability to understand behavior in terms of underlying mental states—thoughts, feelings, desires, intentions—in both oneself and others. Also called reflective functioning, mentalization is a core capacity associated with secure attachment and is one of the strongest protective factors against the intergenerational transmission of insecure attachment.
Instead of reacting to a partner's withdrawal with "They don't care," mentalizing might sound like "I wonder if they're feeling overwhelmed right now."
Main, Mary
American developmental psychologist who expanded Ainsworth's attachment classifications to include the disorganized category, developed the Adult Attachment Interview, and made foundational contributions to the study of attachment across the lifespan.
N
Neural Reconsolidation
The process by which existing memories, when reactivated, become temporarily labile and can be updated with new information before being re-stored. This mechanism underlies many therapeutic approaches to attachment healing, including EMDR and somatic experiencing.
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to form new neural connections, strengthen existing ones, and reorganize its structure and function throughout the lifespan. Neuroplasticity is the biological basis for the possibility of earned secure attachment—the evidence that early attachment patterns can be reshaped through new relational experiences and intentional practice.
Nonviolent Communication (NVC)
A communication framework developed by Marshall Rosenberg that structures expression around four components: observation (what happened, without judgment), feeling (what you feel), need (the universal need beneath the feeling), and request (a specific, doable action). NVC helps translate attachment needs into relationally effective language.
P
Pendulation
A somatic experiencing technique involving the gentle oscillation of attention between a place of activation (discomfort) and a place of resource (calm) in the body. Pendulation teaches the nervous system that it can move fluidly between activation and regulation.
Polyvagal Theory
A theory developed by Stephen Porges that describes three hierarchical states of the autonomic nervous system: ventral vagal (social engagement and safety), sympathetic (fight or flight), and dorsal vagal (freeze or collapse). The theory illuminates how the nervous system's neuroception of safety or danger underlies attachment behaviors.
Protest Behaviors
Actions taken by an anxiously attached individual to reestablish connection with an attachment figure perceived as withdrawing. Includes excessive calling or texting, emotional escalation, threats to leave, and attention-seeking. These are adult expressions of the infant's attachment cry.
Proximity-Seeking
The instinctive drive to maintain closeness to an attachment figure, particularly during times of distress. One of the core attachment behaviors identified by Bowlby, proximity-seeking is expressed differently across attachment styles but is a fundamental feature of the attachment system in all humans.
R
Rejection Sensitivity
A cognitive-emotional disposition characterized by the tendency to anxiously expect, readily perceive, and overreact to social rejection. Strongly associated with anxious attachment and thought to develop from early experiences of inconsistent caregiving.
Repair Attempt
Any gesture—verbal or nonverbal, large or small—that one partner makes during or after conflict to de-escalate tension and restore the felt sense of connection. John Gottman's research identifies the success of repair attempts as the single most important predictor of relationship stability.
A gentle touch on the arm during a heated discussion, a softening of tone, or the words "Can we start over?" are all repair attempts.
Rupture
Any break in the felt sense of connection between two people, ranging from minor misattunements to significant conflicts. In healthy relationships, ruptures are followed by repair. The cycle of rupture and repair is the mechanism through which secure attachment is both tested and strengthened.
S
Secure Attachment
An attachment pattern characterized by comfort with intimacy and interdependence, confidence in the availability of attachment figures, effective emotional regulation, and the ability to use relationships as a secure base from which to explore the world. Develops in response to consistently responsive, attuned caregiving.
Secure Base
Mary Ainsworth's term for the function served by a reliable attachment figure: a safe haven to return to when distressed and a foundation from which to explore the world with confidence. In self-attachment work, the goal is to develop an internal secure base.
Self-Compassion
As defined by Kristin Neff, self-compassion has three components: self-kindness (treating yourself with warmth rather than judgment), common humanity (recognizing suffering as a shared human experience), and mindfulness (holding painful feelings with balanced awareness). Self-compassion activates the caregiving system and supports the development of internal security.
Somatic Experiencing (SE)
A body-oriented therapeutic approach developed by Peter Levine for resolving trauma and chronic stress. SE focuses on tracking and completing the body's unresolved physiological stress responses through careful attention to internal sensations (the felt sense), rather than primarily working with cognitive narratives.
Stonewalling
A conflict behavior in which one partner withdraws from interaction, becoming emotionally and sometimes physically unavailable. Identified by Gottman as one of the "Four Horsemen" that predict relationship dissolution. Often an expression of avoidant deactivation under relational stress.
Strange Situation
A research procedure developed by Mary Ainsworth to assess the quality of attachment between a caregiver and an infant (typically twelve months old). The procedure involves a series of separations and reunions in an unfamiliar room, with attachment classification based primarily on the infant's behavior upon reunion.
V
Ventral Vagal System
The newest branch of the vagus nerve (in polyvagal theory), associated with social engagement, feelings of safety, and the capacity for connection. Activation of the ventral vagal system supports co-regulation, open communication, and relational presence. Many attachment-healing practices aim to increase ventral vagal tone.
W
Window of Tolerance
A concept introduced by Daniel Siegel describing the optimal zone of arousal within which a person can function effectively—processing emotions, thinking clearly, and engaging in relationships. Above the window is hyperarousal; below is hypoarousal. A primary goal of attachment-focused therapy is to widen the window of tolerance.
A person with a narrow window of tolerance may shift rapidly from calm to panic (hyperarousal) or from engagement to numbness (hypoarousal) in response to relational stress.
